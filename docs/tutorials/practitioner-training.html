ETS Clearing Tool · Practitioner Training

Using the ETS Tool for Real Decisions

A trainer-led course for the people who will actually use this model — compliance, policy, trading, strategy, and analysis teams. Organised by the job you're doing, not by the menu.

What this training achieves

Most documentation explains features. This course explains decisions. By the end you will be able to take a real question from your desk — "what will compliance cost us?", "will this cap design calm the price?", "when does fuel-switching pay off?" — and turn it into a scenario the tool can answer, then read the answer correctly and defend it.

Who this is for We teach to five practitioner roles. Pick the modules that match your job; the matrix at the end maps every feature to the roles that use it.
  • Compliance & carbon managers at regulated firms — cost forecasting, banking, border exposure.
  • Policy analysts & regulators — cap design, market stabilisers, auction revenue.
  • Market analysts & traders — price-path outlook, banking dynamics, market power.
  • Corporate strategy & transition planners — abatement economics, technology choice, benchmarking.
  • Quantitative & research analysts — economic feedback, model coupling, calibration, sensitivity.
Learning outcomes
  • Frame a business question as a scenario: cap, supply, participants, behaviour.
  • Choose the right features for the question — and know which assumptions you are buying.
  • Run a scenario and read the output columns that matter for your decision.
  • Compare designs (policy vs policy, approach vs approach) and quantify the trade-off.
  • Know the model's boundary — what it does not tell you — so you don't over-claim.
How to use this course Each module is a self-contained ~15-minute unit: a brief (the situation), the questions you must answer, the features in play, a build you run yourself, how to read the result, and a your-turn exercise. Every config maps to a file in examples/, so you can open the full scenario and tinker. Prerequisite: skim the walkthrough so you know how to run a scenario (python ets_framework.py --config <file>.json). Keep the cookbook open as a feature reference.
One thing to internalise first This is a partial-equilibrium model of the allowance market: it solves the carbon price where demand meets supply, holding the wider economy fixed. That makes it fast, transparent, and honest about the carbon market — but output prices, GDP, and energy demand are inputs, not results, unless you switch on the feedback channels in Module 5. Whenever you present a number, know which side of that line it sits on.

1

Compliance & cost planning

Regulated firm
The briefYou run carbon compliance for a steel plant inside a tightening cap. Finance wants a five-year cost forecast and a recommendation: do we bank allowances now while they're cheap, and how exposed are we if a border tariff (CBAM) lands on our exports?

Questions you must answer: What's our annual compliance cost as the cap declines? Is it cheaper to abate, buy, or bank? What does CBAM add?

Features: MAC curvesfree allocation banking / borrowingexpectation_ruleCBAM

Step 1 — Build the firm and the cap path.

Give the plant a MAC curve and a declining cap, with banking allowed so it can pre-buy. A rising expectation_rule signal is what makes banking rational.

"banking_allowed": true,
"expectation_rule": "perfect_foresight",
"participants": [
  {"name": "Steel", "initial_emissions": 180, "free_allocation_ratio": 0.5,
   "penalty_price": 300, "abatement_type": "linear", "max_abatement": 54, "cost_slope": 2.6}
]

Step 2 — Add border exposure.

"eua_price": 95.0,            // reference (export-market) carbon price, per year
"cbam_export_share": 0.35,    // 35% of output exported into the CBAM zone
"cbam_coverage_ratio": 1.0

Step 3 — Run a representative scenario and read the cost.

python ets_framework.py --config examples/climate_solutions_banking_borrowing.json

How to read it

ColumnWhat it tells finance
Total Compliance CostThe headline number — abatement + allowance purchases + penalties − sales.
Abatement vs Allowance BuysHow much of compliance is "do it ourselves" vs "buy from the market".
Banked Allowances / Ending Bank BalanceWhether pre-buying is optimal at the expected price path.
Total CBAM LiabilityThe extra owed on exports — money that leaves with the goods.
Instructor noteThe firm banks only when the model expects a higher future price (expected_future_price > current). If banking shows zero, your price path isn't rising fast enough to justify it — that's a finding, not a bug.
Your turnHalve free_allocation_ratio to 0.25 (a phase-out) and re-run. How much does Total Compliance Cost rise, and does the firm now abate more or buy more? Then set cbam_export_share to 0 — how much of the cost was border exposure?
2

Policy design & price stability

Regulator
The briefYou're designing the next ETS phase. The cap must decline to hit a target, but the last phase saw violent price swings that scared off investment. You need a cap pathway and a stabiliser — and you must show the Treasury what happens to auction revenue.

Questions: What declining cap hits the target? Does a Market Stability Reserve or a Carbon Cap Rule calm the price more? What's the revenue, and how much leaks abroad via CBAM?

Features: cap_trajectoryMSRCCR auction revenueprice floor/ceiling

Step 1 — Set the cap pathway once (not per year).

"cap_trajectory": {"start_year": "2026", "end_year": "2035", "start_value": 500, "end_value": 300}

Step 2 — Compare two stabilisers on the same market.

Quantity valve (MSR) vs adaptive cap (CCR). Run each against a fixed-cap baseline:

python ets_framework.py --config examples/climate_solutions_msr_stability.json   # MSR on vs off
python ets_framework.py --config examples/benmir_ccr_carbon_cap_rule.json        # CCR vs fixed

How to read it

SignalDecision use
Std-dev of Equilibrium Carbon Price across yearsYour stability metric. The CCR example cuts it ~27% vs a fixed cap; compare to the MSR run.
MSR Withheld / Released / Reserve PoolHow aggressively the reserve is intervening.
CCR Cap Adjustment / Cost DeviationWhere the adaptive cap loosened (costs hot) or tightened (emissions high).
Total Auction Revenue, CBAM Foregone RevenueThe Treasury view — domestic take vs revenue lost across the border.
Instructor noteMSR acts on the banked surplus (a stock); CCR acts on emissions and abatement-cost gaps (flows), one year lagged. They answer different worries — surplus overhang vs cost-shock volatility. Reach for the CCR doc and MSR doc before recommending one.
Your turnIn the CCR example, set ccr_phi_abatement_cost to 0 (respond to emissions only) and re-run. How much of the volatility reduction did the cost term provide? That number is your argument for or against a cost-responsive cap.
3

Price outlook & trading

Market analyst
The briefYour desk needs a defensible price-path view for the next decade. You also worry that one or two dominant emitters could move the market. You want to see how much the assumption about how prices form changes your outlook.

Questions: What shape is the price path under intertemporal optimisation? What if banking is Hotelling-style? What if a big player exercises market power? How different are the answers?

Features: expectation_ruleHotelling Nash–Cournotmodel_approach: all

Step 1 — Pick the price-formation lens.

// intertemporal arbitrage: price rises at the discount (+ risk) rate
"model_approach": "hotelling", "discount_rate": 0.055, "risk_premium": 0.0,
// strategic withholding by a dominant player:
"model_approach": "nash_cournot", "nash_strategic_participants": ["Power"]

Step 2 — Run all three lenses at once and compare.

"model_approach": "all"   // emits [Competitive], [Hotelling], [Nash-Cournot] result sets

How to read it

  • Competitive — the price-taker benchmark; your central case.
  • Hotelling — a smoothly rising path tied to the discount rate; needs a carbon_budget. Use when banking dominates.
  • Nash–Cournot — the strategic firm abates less and lifts the price; the gap to competitive is the market-power premium.
Instructor noteThe spread across the three approaches is your model-risk band. If the trade thesis only works under one approach, say so explicitly. A Nash run may log a non-convergence warning — treat that result as indicative, not precise.
Your turnTake any banking example, set model_approach: "all", and tabulate the three price paths. Which assumption matters most for your market — and why?

Transition & technology investment

Corporate strategy
The briefYou advise a cement group on its decarbonisation roadmap. At what carbon price does each lever — efficiency, fuel switch, CCS — become economic? Some efficiency upgrades pay for themselves today. And under output-based allocation, how much free allocation does the benchmark grant?

Questions: Which abatement levers, in what order, at what price? Which are "no-regret" today? How does benchmarked allocation change the picture?

Features: piecewise MACnegative-cost blocks technology switchingOBA benchmark

Step 1 — Encode the abatement levers as a MAC stack (no-regret first).

"abatement_type": "piecewise",
"mac_blocks": [
  {"amount": 5,  "marginal_cost": -40},   // efficiency: net-saving, do it now
  {"amount": 8,  "marginal_cost":  35},   // fuel switch
  {"amount": 6,  "marginal_cost": 120}    // CCS: only at a high price
]

Step 2 — Or let the model choose between whole technologies.

python ets_framework.py --config examples/climate_solutions_technology_switching.json

Step 3 — Add benchmarked free allocation (OBA).

python ets_framework.py --config examples/k_ets_oba_benchmark.json

How to read it

ColumnStrategy read
Abatement at each priceThe investment trigger price for each lever (block crossed).
Chosen Technology / Technology MixThe least-cost technology portfolio at the prevailing price.
Free AllocationUnder OBA = intensity × output; below benchmark ⇒ you're a net seller.
Instructor noteNegative-cost blocks are taken even at a zero carbon price — they're profitable on their own. The roadmap is: do those immediately, then watch for the price to cross each higher block. See the MAC doc for how the curve maps to real measures.
Your turnMove the CCS block from 120 to 80 (a cost breakthrough). At what scenario price does the firm now adopt CCS, and how much does total abatement jump?

Economic feedback & scenario analysis

Senior analyst
The briefLeadership wants a Base / Middle / Ideal outlook to 2040 and is sceptical of "runaway price" headlines. You need to show what happens when firms can cut output in response to price (not just abate), and — for the research note — couple the carbon price to an energy-system view.

Questions: Does letting activity respond cap the price? What's the multi-scenario spread? How do I close the loop with an external model without rebuilding one here?

Features: feedback A: price-elastic baselinefeedback B: coupling multi-scenarioK-ETS Outlook

Step 1 — Switch on demand destruction (feedback A).

"reference_carbon_price": 55.0,         // scenario anchor
// per participant:
"output_price_elasticity": 0.5          // activity contracts as price rises above the anchor
python ets_framework.py --config examples/feedback_a_price_elastic_baseline.json

Step 2 — A real multi-scenario outlook.

python ets_framework.py --config examples/climate_solutions_k_ets_outlook.json   # Base / Middle / Ideal

Step 3 — Close the loop with an external model (feedback B, Python).

from ets.coupling import run_coupled_simulation, ElasticityExternalModel
result = run_coupled_simulation(config,
    ElasticityExternalModel(elasticity=0.5, reference_price=55.0),
    max_iterations=40, tolerance=0.25, relaxation=0.5)
print(result.converged, result.iterations)   # swap in your own model via .respond()

How to read it

  • Compare the elastic vs inelastic price path — the gap is the "demand destruction" cushion against price spikes.
  • In the elastic run, the per-firm Initial Emissions column shows the price-scaled activity.
  • For coupling, watch result.price_history converge; the loop solves the joint ETS↔economy equilibrium.
Instructor note · know the boundaryFeedback A is reduced-form, own-price — still partial equilibrium. Real cross-sector and macro effects need a real model behind feedback B's adapter. Don't present a feedback-A run as general-equilibrium. See feedback A and feedback B.
Your turnIn the feedback-A example, raise output_price_elasticity from 0.5 to 1.0. How much further does the 2030 price fall, and how much does activity (the scaled Initial Emissions) contract? Where does the response saturate?
6

Calibration & sensitivity

Quant / research
The briefBefore anyone trusts the model, it must reproduce history. You have an observed price series and need to fit the firms' abatement costs to it — then stress the result across a grid of assumptions for the committee.

Questions: What MAC slopes reproduce the observed prices? How sensitive are outcomes to the reference price, the cap, or an elasticity? Where are the cliffs?

Features: /api/calibrate/api/batch-run

Step 1 — Calibrate MAC slopes to observed prices (Nelder–Mead).

POST /api/calibrate
{ "config": {…}, "observed_prices": {"2021": 33, "2022": 81, …},
  "participant_names": ["Power", "Steel"] }
# template payload: examples/k_ets_calibration_request.json

Step 2 — Sweep the assumptions you can't pin down.

POST /api/batch-run
{ "config": {…},
  "sweeps": [ {"path": "…eua_price", "values": [70, 90, 110]},
              {"path": "…total_cap", "values": [450, 400, 350]} ] }
# template payload: examples/k_ets_batch_eua_sweep.json

How to read it

  • Calibration returns the fitted cost_slope per named firm and a residual error — your goodness-of-fit.
  • Batch run returns one result row per parameter combination — plot price/abatement across the grid to find thresholds and non-linearities.
Instructor noteCalibration fits the linear MAC slope only; piecewise blocks aren't auto-fitted. Method & payload schemas: analysis-tools doc. These two run via the API (or the editor), not --config.
Your turnDesign a 3×3 sweep (cap × reference price) for your own market and identify the combination where the price first breaches a level your stakeholders care about.
